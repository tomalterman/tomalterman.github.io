CRP

  • Date Friday, May 17, 2013
  • Value 13.6 mg/L
About This Test

CRP is a protein in your blood that rises when there is inflammation in the body. Scientists were very excited when they discovered it because its a more direct test than ESR for detecting inflammation. Measuring CRP is a way of detecting how much inflammatory activity is going on in the body. For certain inflammatory conditions, high CRP can be a way of measuring if a disease is becoming more active.

Hemoglobin

About This Test

Hemoglobin is a protein that carries oxygen around in your blood stream and delivers it to cells through out your body. When hemoglobin is low (also called anemia), not as much oxygen gets to your tissues and you may feel tired or easily short of breath. The most common causes for low hemoglobin are not eating or absorbing enough of certain vitamin/minerals, blood loss, or chronic disease.

Red Blood Cells

4.36

Red Blood Cells

About This Test

Red blood cells (RBCs) are the part of your blood that carry around oxygen and deliver it to your tissues. This test measures the number of RBCs in your blood. Without enough RBCs (which is called anemia), your body isn't getting the oxygen it needs and you may feel tired or easily short of breath. There are lots of reasons why RBC counts can be off. Some examples are that RBCs can be low if you're not absorbing enough nutrients from food or if you're having any bleeding or clotting problems, and high RBCs can mean dehydration or as a side effect of certain medications.

23.6

Lymphocytes (%)

About This Test

Lymphocytes are a type of white blood cell (WBC). While it's important to check your number of WBCs, it can also be helpful to see how many of each kind of WBC you have. Because of that, this count can be shown either in numbers or by percentage of WBCs. High lymphocytes often show up in people with infections of any kind, but mostly commonly viral infections. Low lymphocytes signal an increased susceptibility to infection.

TSH

TSH

About This Test

TSH stands for Thyroid Stimulating Hormone and its job is to signal the thyroid gland to make and release more thyroid hormone. In healthy people, this test is commonly used to screen for any thyroid problems. When thyroid hormone levels are low, TSH goes up because the brain tries to tell the thyroid to speed up. In other words, high levels of TSH suggest that you may have an under-active thyroid gland, while low levels indicate that your thyroid could be overactive.

MCV

94.5

MCV

About This Test

MCV is a test that measures the size of your red blood cells. Size is important because red blood cells that are too large or too small don't carry oxygen as well as they should. If you have anemia (where your blood isn't carrying enough oxygen to your tissues), MCV can give important clues as to the cause. For example, a low MCV can mean that you are iron deficient, while a high MCV might mean that you are low in Vitamin B12 or Folate.

RDW

About This Test

RDW measures how much variation there is between the size of your red blood cells. It is another important clue in figuring out the cause of anemia, when your blood isn't carrying around enough oxygen for your tissues. For example, some forms of anemia cause you to make both normal sized red blood cells and tiny sized red blood cells. When this happens, you'll see a high RDW because of the big difference between the sizes of those cells.

ESR

5

ESR

About This Test

ESR measures inflammation in the body. Inflammation causes increased amounts of a protein that helps red blood cells stick together. When red blood cells stick together, these clumps become heavy and sink to the bottom of a test tube. ESR measures the rate at which these clumps sink down and separate from other parts of the blood. When you have inflammation, these clumps form and sink quickly, causing a high ESR. For certain inflammatory conditions, high ESR can be a way of measuring if a disease is becoming more active.

Hematocrit

41.2

Hematocrit

About This Test

Hematocrit measures how much of your blood is made up of red blood cells. Red blood cells carry oxygen in the blood, so when hematocrit becomes low (also called anemia), you may feel tired or easily short of breath. Although lots of things can affect your levels, the most common causes of low hematocrit are not eating or absorbing enough of certain vitamins/minerals, blood loss, or chronic disease.

Albumin

4.4

Albumin

About This Test

Albumin is a protein in your blood that helps other proteins move around and regulates blood volume. For patients with chronic disease, low albumin can suggest that the person is either losing protein because their disease is more active or they are having trouble absorbing protein from their food (or both). Albumin is also used as a marker of inflammation. Inflammation in the body causes albumin to be used up quickly leading to lower levels of albumin in the blood.

White Blood Cells

About This Test

White blood cells are immune system cells that travel around your body and respond to anything it doesn't recognize as “normal”, such as infection or inflammation. Higher than normal levels of WBCs happen most commonly with infection, inflammation, or dehydration. Low levels of WBCs frequently occur with viral infections, autoimmune conditions, or when taking certain medications.

Platelets

125

Platelets

About This Test

Platelets are a part of your blood that help you form clots when you're bleeding. There are many causes for both high and low levels. This test is used to make sure that you have a healthy number of platelets needed for proper blood clotting. Too few platelets and your risk of bleeding increases, while too many platelets can make you more likely to form unhealthy clots.
